Reading list

Segmentation Papers

This reading list is organized for students moving from classical segmentation architectures toward transformer models, self-supervised learning, and promptable foundation models. Each entry includes a summary and what to focus on when reading.

How to use this list: Start with the Foundational group. Papers marked Foundational introduce core ideas that all later work builds on. Read the abstracts and key sections of every paper in the foundational group before moving to transformers or self-supervised methods.

Foundational Segmentation Papers

These papers introduced the architectural ideas, training recipes, and evaluation standards that define modern segmentation. Every segmentation student should be familiar with all four.

2015

Start here

Fully Convolutional Networks for Semantic Segmentation

Introduced end-to-end dense prediction by converting classification CNNs into fully convolutional networks for pixel-wise labeling. Showed that any CNN can be repurposed as a dense predictor.

What to focus on: The upsampling technique (transposed convolution / bilinear interpolation), skip connections from earlier layers, and mean IoU evaluation on PASCAL VOC. Understand why removing the fully connected layers enables arbitrary input size.

Read paper →

2015

Start here

U-Net: Convolutional Networks for Biomedical Image Segmentation

Established the encoder-decoder architecture with skip connections for precise biomedical segmentation from small annotated datasets. Foundational for medical, agricultural, and small-data segmentation.

What to focus on: The symmetric encoder-decoder with concatenated skip connections, the use of data augmentation to train with few images, and why skip connections recover fine spatial detail lost during pooling. The U-Net design remains the dominant architecture in medical imaging.

Read paper →

2017

Start here

Mask R-CNN

Extended Faster R-CNN with a parallel mask branch, introducing RoIAlign for precise spatial alignment. Made instance-level masks a natural extension of detection-based pipelines.

What to focus on: RoIAlign vs. RoIPool (the alignment problem), the parallel mask head architecture, how box detection and mask prediction use different loss formulations, and the Mask AP evaluation methodology. Mask R-CNN is the baseline every instance segmentation paper is compared against.

Read paper →

2019

Intermediate

Panoptic Segmentation

Formalized a unified task that combines semantic segmentation for stuff regions with instance segmentation for countable things — and introduced the Panoptic Quality metric.

What to focus on: The definition of things vs. stuff, the merging logic that resolves conflicts between semantic and instance predictions, and the Panoptic Quality formula (SQ × RQ). This is the right starting point for understanding why semantic and instance outputs need reconciliation.

Read paper →

Transformer-Based Segmentation Papers

These papers brought attention mechanisms and hierarchical vision transformers to segmentation, improving performance and enabling universal models that handle multiple tasks.

2021

Intermediate

SegFormer

Combined a hierarchical transformer encoder with a lightweight all-MLP decoder for efficient semantic segmentation. Achieves strong performance without complex decoder designs.

What to focus on: The Mix Transformer (MiT) backbone and how it produces hierarchical features at multiple scales, the simple MLP decoder that replaces complex ASPP or FPN modules, and the robustness analysis across common corruptions. SegFormer is a clean example of why transformers simplify decoder design.

Read paper →

2021

Intermediate

Swin Transformer

Introduced shifted-window self-attention with a hierarchical feature pyramid — making vision transformers practical as dense prediction backbones without quadratic attention cost.

What to focus on: Local window attention vs. global attention (computational cost comparison), the shifted-window mechanism that allows cross-window information flow, and the hierarchical patch merging that creates multi-scale features. Swin is a backbone paper, not primarily a segmentation paper, but it underpins Mask2Former and many other modern models.

Read paper →

2022

Intermediate

Mask2Former

Unified semantic, instance, and panoptic segmentation using masked attention and a shared mask classification formulation. One architecture family achieves top results across all three tasks.

What to focus on: The mask classification formulation that reframes segmentation as a set prediction problem, masked attention that constrains cross-attention to predicted mask regions, and how the same model handles all three segmentation tasks by changing only the label supervision. Mask2Former is the standard modern universal segmentation baseline.

Read paper →

Self-Supervised Learning Papers

These papers show how to pretrain strong visual encoders without segmentation labels — useful for low-annotation domains like medical imaging and agricultural analysis.

2020

Intermediate

SimCLR

Popularized simple contrastive learning for visual representation learning using augmentation-based positive pairs and a projection head.

What to focus on: The NT-Xent contrastive loss, the role of the projection head in improving downstream transfer, and the sensitivity to augmentation choice and batch size. SimCLR established that contrastive pretraining can significantly improve segmentation encoders when labeled data is scarce.

Read paper →

2021

Intermediate

DINO: Emerging Properties in Self-Supervised Vision Transformers

Showed that self-distilled ViTs learn object-centric representations and produce attention maps that closely follow object boundaries — without any segmentation supervision.

What to focus on: The self-distillation training scheme (student and momentum teacher), the emergence of semantic object regions in the attention heads of the last layer, and how DINO features can be used directly for unsupervised segmentation and pseudo-mask generation. This paper is especially relevant for low-annotation segmentation workflows.

Read paper →

2022

Advanced

Masked Autoencoders Are Scalable Vision Learners (MAE)

Demonstrated that masking 75% of image patches and reconstructing them is a highly efficient pretraining strategy for vision transformers at scale.

What to focus on: The asymmetric encoder-decoder design that processes only visible patches, the very high masking ratio that forces global reconstruction, and transfer performance to segmentation (ADE20K) vs. classification. MAE pretraining produces rich spatial features that benefit dense prediction tasks.

Read paper →

Segment Anything and Foundation Model Papers

These papers introduce promptable, class-agnostic segmentation models trained at large scale. They are especially relevant for annotation workflows, zero-shot segmentation, and video understanding.

2023

Intermediate

Segment Anything (SAM)

Introduced a promptable segmentation foundation model trained on over 1 billion masks. Accepts point, box, or text prompts and returns masks without task-specific training.

What to focus on: The promptable interface design, the SA-1B dataset construction pipeline, and — critically — that SAM predicts masks but does not assign semantic class labels. Students often confuse SAM-generated masks with labeled ground truth. SAM is a segmentation oracle, not a semantic classifier.

Read paper →

2023

Intermediate

Fast Segment Anything (FastSAM)

Proposed a faster CNN-based alternative to SAM that frames promptable segmentation as all-instance segmentation followed by prompt-guided selection.

What to focus on: The speed-accuracy trade-off vs. SAM-ViT-H, how the YOLACT-style pipeline is adapted for all-instance generation, and the limitations of the simplified approach for complex scenes. FastSAM is useful for resource-constrained annotation workflows.

Read paper →

2024

Advanced

SAM 2: Segment Anything in Images and Videos

Extended SAM from images to video using a streaming memory architecture that propagates prompted masks across frames — enabling interactive video object segmentation.

What to focus on: The streaming memory bank that stores frame-level context, how prompts from a single frame are propagated forward and backward in time, and the SA-V dataset used for training. SAM 2 is the reference for understanding how promptable image segmentation scales to temporal sequences.

Read paper →
Exam and review tip: For each paper you read, be able to answer: (1) What problem does it solve that previous work could not? (2) What is the core architectural idea? (3) What metric does it report and on what benchmark? (4) What are the known limitations? These four questions cover most exam and research review questions about segmentation papers.
